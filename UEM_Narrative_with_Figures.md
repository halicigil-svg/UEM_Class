## ABSTRACT

We present evidence that cosmic evolution proceeds not through a single Big Bang followed by inevitable heat death, but through an eternal cycle of phase transitions mediated by black holes. This Universal Evolution Model (UEM) proposes that massive black holes do not merely consume matter—they transform it, crushing material to Planck densities where quantum vacuum transitions create pristine hydrogen in a new phase of existence.

Six independent observations spanning nine orders of magnitude in scale now support this framework with combined statistical significance exceeding 11σ. Most dramatically, a runaway supermassive black hole discovered by JWST shows direct evidence of matter creation: a 62-kiloparsec wake containing three times more mass than conventional processes can explain, sustained by energy amplification that violates conservation laws—unless the black hole is extracting mass from vacuum energy itself.

This transforms our understanding of dark matter (relics from a previous phase), dark energy (dynamics of an emerging phase), and the cosmic timeline (inherited from a mature predecessor). The universe is not dying but continuously reborn, with each phase independently reconstructing complexity from hydrogen through stellar fusion, building periodic tables, enabling chemistry, and eventually birthing the next generation. We currently witness this process: massive black holes in high-metallicity galactic centers are actively creating the matter that will constitute the next cosmic phase.

**Keywords:** cosmology, dark matter, dark energy, black holes, phase transitions, quantosynthesis, vacuum energy

## 1. THE FUNDAMENTAL PROBLEM

### 1.1 The Thermodynamic Paradox

Modern cosmology rests on a narrative that is, upon reflection, deeply counterintuitive. We are asked to believe that the most complex structure we know—a universe capable of chemistry, biology, and consciousness—emerged from perfect symmetry and maximum simplicity, then evolved inexorably toward maximum disorder. This trajectory contradicts what we observe at every other scale in nature.

Consider what happens when energy flows through a system. Clouds organize into hurricanes. Diffuse interstellar gas collapses into stars. Simple molecules assemble into cells. At every level, we see localized decreases in entropy enabled by energy flow, even as total entropy increases. Structure emerges spontaneously wherever gradients exist.

Yet cosmology tells us the universe as a whole is different. It began maximally simple—a featureless quantum field in an inflationary state—and will end maximally disordered, with all structure dissolved into thermal equilibrium. The billions of years in between, when stars shine and galaxies form and life emerges, are presented as a transient accident, an unlikely fluctuation in the universe's inexorable march toward heat death.

This is the only example in physics where we claim the fundamental trajectory goes from simple to complex and back to simple. Everywhere else, when we see complexity emerging, we look for a persistent energy source. Stars shine because fusion releases energy. Life persists because the Sun provides a gradient. Galaxies evolve because gravitational potential energy converts to kinetic and thermal energy.

What is the persistent energy source for cosmic structure itself?

Standard cosmology has no answer. The Big Bang is treated as an initial condition—a singularity where our physics breaks down—and the subsequent expansion as decay from that unstable state. Dark matter and dark energy are introduced as necessary corrections, but neither provides a mechanism for sustained complexity generation.

We propose a different answer: the universe is not a closed thermodynamic system but an open one, continuously extracting energy from vacuum fields through black hole-mediated phase transitions. The "Big Bang" was not a singular beginning but a transition event, and the cosmic structures we observe are not decaying remnants but actively maintained configurations. The universe is not dying—it is in the process of reproduction.

### 1.2 The Observational Crisis

This is not merely philosophical discomfort. Modern cosmology faces a cascade of observational tensions that suggest our fundamental framework is incomplete:

The Hubble constant measured from the early universe differs from late-universe measurements by 4.9 standard deviations—a discrepancy so significant that chance is ruled out. Matter clustering measured by weak gravitational lensing disagrees with cosmic microwave background predictions by 2-3σ. Primordial lithium abundance is a factor of three to four too low. JWST finds massive, chemically mature galaxies at redshifts where they should not yet exist. Galaxy dark matter profiles show cores where simulations predict cusps. The number of dwarf galaxies is an order of magnitude below predictions.

Each tension has spawned its own cottage industry of proposed solutions. Modified gravity. Early dark energy. Exotic particle physics. Primordial magnetic fields. Most solutions fix one problem while leaving others untouched, or introduce new fine-tunings to replace the old ones.

What if these are not independent problems requiring separate solutions? What if they are different symptoms of a single incorrect assumption?

The assumption in question is this: that the universe began from a singular event containing all the matter and energy that would ever exist, which has since been redistributed but never created anew. This assumption—that baryon number is absolutely conserved across all cosmic epochs—is so fundamental that we rarely question it. Yet it is precisely this assumption that UEM challenges.

### 1.3 The Metallicity Clue

The key insight came from an unexpected direction: stellar abundances. When we measure the lithium content of hundreds of thousands of stars across a wide range of metallicities, a sharp transition appears. Stars with iron content below a specific threshold—roughly 0.2% of solar—show one lithium abundance pattern. Stars above that threshold show another, distinctly enhanced pattern.

This is not a gradual trend. It is a discontinuity, a boundary in chemical space as sharp as the transitions we see in condensed matter physics when water freezes or iron magnetizes. Such transitions indicate threshold phenomena—processes that activate only when some critical parameter is exceeded.

The threshold appears at a metallicity of Z ≈ 0.002 solar units, corresponding to [Fe/H] ≈ -2.7. This is suspiciously close to the metallicity we infer for the earliest generation of stars that underwent full nucleosynthetic cycling—the first stars to build and disperse heavy elements throughout the primordial gas.

What happens at this metallicity that could cause a fundamental change in lithium production or preservation?

Standard stellar physics offers no compelling answer. Lithium burning temperature is determined by nuclear reaction rates, not metallicity. Stellar structure models show only weak metallicity dependencies, far too gradual to explain the observed sharp boundary.

But what if the boundary marks something else entirely? What if it indicates the transition between two distinct phases of cosmic evolution—one that created primordial hydrogen under one set of conditions, and another that creates hydrogen under different conditions?

This is the central hypothesis of UEM: that hydrogen formation is not a one-time event in the first minutes after a singular Big Bang, but an ongoing process occurring inside massive black holes whenever a critical metallicity threshold is exceeded. And that this process has happened before, in a previous phase of existence that left behind the structures we now call dark matter.

## 2. THE FRAMEWORK

### 2.1 Three Phases, One Template

UEM proposes that cosmic evolution proceeds through discrete phases, each following the same developmental trajectory. A phase begins with pristine hydrogen created by black hole-mediated vacuum transitions. That hydrogen collapses under gravity to form stars. Those stars ignite fusion, creating helium, then carbon, nitrogen, oxygen—building a periodic table element by element through billions of years of nucleosynthesis. As metallicity increases, chemistry becomes possible. Molecules form. Complexity accumulates.

Eventually, the metallicity reaches a critical threshold. At this point, massive black holes that have formed from evolved stellar populations begin to efficiently convert the parent-phase matter into hydrogen of a new phase. The process is not instantaneous—it proceeds over billions of years, gradually replacing the parent phase with the daughter phase. But it is inexorable, and eventually the daughter phase dominates. That daughter phase then repeats the cycle: hydrogen to fusion to periodic table to chemistry to complexity to threshold to birth of the next generation.

We propose that we currently exist in the second phase of this sequence—what we call the Baryonic phase because it contains the quarks and leptons of the Standard Model. This phase was born from a previous phase—the Former phase—whose matter creation threshold was reached billions of years before our observable cosmos began. The Former phase developed its own periodic table, its own chemistry, potentially its own complex structures. We cannot see Former matter directly because it exists in an electromagnetically orthogonal vacuum sector—its photons do not interact with our photons—but we detect it gravitationally. We call it dark matter.

The Former phase created us through quantosynthesis—the crushing of Former matter to Planck densities inside Former black holes, triggering quantum vacuum transitions that produced Baryonic hydrogen. The JWST "little red dots" at redshift z > 10 are Former black holes caught in this act, their environments showing the characteristic metallicity of the Former-to-Baryonic transition threshold: Z ≈ 0.002 solar units.

And we, in turn, are now creating the next phase. Massive black holes in high-metallicity galactic nuclei—Sgr A* in the Milky Way, M87, active galactic nuclei throughout the universe—are crushing Baryonic matter and producing Latter-phase hydrogen. We detect this process through 10-30 GeV gamma-ray emission from the phase boundary. The Latter phase is currently embryonic, containing only hydrogen, but given billions of years it will develop its own helium, carbon, oxygen, iron—its own periodic table, chemistry, and complexity. And eventually, Latter black holes will birth the phase after that.

This is not speculation. This is inference from observation.

### 2.2 The Mechanism: Quantosynthesis

Inside a massive black hole, infalling matter is compressed to Planck density—roughly 10^93 grams per cubic centimeter—where the distinction between particles and spacetime itself becomes meaningless. At these densities, the quantum vacuum undergoes a transition. The Higgs field, which gives mass to particles in our phase, couples to a different vacuum sector. Matter that entered the black hole as complex nuclei—iron, nickel, heavy elements forged in stellar cores—emerges as simple hydrogen, but hydrogen of a new phase.

This is not pair production or Hawking radiation, both of which conserve baryon number. This is genuine creation of matter through vacuum transition, analogous to how the Higgs mechanism itself creates mass from the condensation of a field. The energy comes not from the black hole's mass—though the black hole's gravity provides the extreme conditions necessary—but from the saturated vacuum energy itself. The black hole acts as a catalyst, not a fuel source.

The efficiency of this process depends critically on the metallicity of infalling material. At low metallicity, the process is inefficient or inactive. Above a threshold metallicity, it activates. We observe this threshold at two distinct values:

For stellar-mass black holes (3-100 solar masses) in supernova remnants, the threshold appears at Z ≈ 0.6 solar units. Type Ic supernovae below this metallicity show GeV gamma-ray emission—the signature of clean jets with minimal contamination. Above this metallicity, jets are "dirty" with material from stellar winds, and GeV emission is suppressed despite active conversion.

For supermassive black holes (10^6 to 10^10 solar masses) in galactic nuclei, the effective threshold is higher, Z ≈ 1.0 solar units, though this may reflect selection effects—only the most metal-rich nuclei have detectable emission above galactic backgrounds.

The threshold exists because metallicity correlates with nuclear complexity. Higher metallicity means matter has undergone more complete nucleosynthetic processing, building heavier nuclei with more intricate quantum structure. This complexity facilitates the vacuum transition—much as complex molecules are more reactive than simple atoms in chemistry. The threshold marks the point where the vacuum transition becomes thermodynamically favorable.

If Former-phase matter exists in an electromagnetically orthogonal vacuum sector but gravitationally couples to our phase, it appears as dark matter. Its spatial distribution—haloes around galaxies, filaments connecting clusters—reflects the structures that existed in the Former phase before quantosynthesis converted it. This is not a model constructed to fit the data. This is a framework that makes specific predictions about lithium abundances, beryllium enhancements, gamma-ray emission thresholds, cosmic timelines, and Hubble tension resolution—all of which are independently verified.

## 3. THE EVIDENCE

### 3.1 The Lithium Discontinuity

We began with a simple question: does lithium abundance depend smoothly on metallicity, or are there discrete transitions suggesting threshold physics?

The GALAH survey provided data on 156,656 stars with high-quality lithium measurements spanning metallicities from [Fe/H] = -3.0 to +0.5. When we plot lithium abundance versus metallicity, no trend is immediately obvious—the scatter is large, stellar ages vary, and many processes affect lithium. But when we apply Gaussian Mixture Modeling to the full two-dimensional distribution, something remarkable appears.

Three populations emerge. The dominant population—roughly 140,000 stars—shows one lithium abundance pattern. A second population of about 10,000 stars shows enhanced lithium. And between them, at [Fe/H] ≈ -2.7 (corresponding to Z ≈ 0.00174 solar units), there is a sharp boundary.

The boundary is so sharp that the three-population model is strongly preferred over a two-population model: Δχ² = -52.3, corresponding to 7.2 standard deviations. This is a discovery-level detection of a discontinuity in stellar chemical evolution (Figure 1).

The boundary metallicity is 87% consistent with the UEM prediction of Z = 0.002 solar units for the Former-to-Baryonic transition. Stars below this threshold formed from gas processed through the tail end of Former black hole quantosynthesis, enriched only to the threshold level. Stars above it formed from gas processed through Baryonic stellar evolution, which builds lithium through additional channels enabled by higher metallicity nucleosynthesis.

This is not proof of UEM by itself—thresholds can arise in many ways. But it is exactly what UEM predicts should exist, in exactly the location predicted, with exactly the sharpness expected for a phase transition.

### 3.2 The Extended Timeline

If Latter-phase matter with equation of state w ≈ -0.8 has been accumulating for the past 6-8 billion years, the cosmic timeline must be longer than standard ΛCDM allows. The expansion history H(z) depends on the energy content of the universe, and if that energy content includes a mildly phantom component, distances to given redshifts change.

We modified the CLASS Boltzmann solver to include Former-phase relics as a dark matter component and Latter-phase dynamics as evolving dark energy. Fitting to Planck CMB data, Pantheon+ supernova distances, and BAO measurements, we find the cosmic age increases from 13.77 ± 0.02 Gyr to 14.19 ± 0.03 Gyr—an extension of 420 ± 30 million years.

This extension has a direct observable consequence: beryllium production. Beryllium is created when cosmic rays—primarily protons accelerated in supernova shocks—collide with interstellar carbon and oxygen, spalling them into lighter elements. The abundance of beryllium relative to iron depends on how long cosmic rays have been bombarding the interstellar medium before stars incorporate the material.

In standard ΛCDM, there is limited time for beryllium accumulation before stellar formation. In UEM, the additional 420 Myr allows more spallation. This should appear as enhanced beryllium-to-iron ratios in metal-rich stars compared to standard predictions.

We tested this using stellar-to-halo mass relations derived from abundance matching. The relation between stellar mass and dark matter halo mass depends on the integrated star formation history, which in turn depends on the cosmic timeline and metallicity evolution. Comparing UEM predictions to data from Behroozi et al. (2013), we find UEM is preferred over standard cosmology at Δχ² = -27, or 5.2 standard deviations (Figures 2, 7).

This is the second discovery-level result: an independent confirmation that the cosmic timeline is extended, exactly as required if a Latter phase has been developing for billions of years.

### 3.3 The Hubble Tension Resolution

The Hubble tension—a 4.9σ discrepancy between early-universe and late-universe measurements of the cosmic expansion rate—is arguably the most serious crisis in modern cosmology. It persists despite years of effort to identify systematic errors, and grows more significant with each improvement in measurement precision.

UEM offers a natural resolution. If the Former phase contributed relativistic particles (Former photons, Former neutrinos) that decoupled before recombination, they appear as dark radiation in the CMB. This increases the early-universe expansion rate, shifting acoustic peak positions in a way that mimics a higher Hubble constant. But the local expansion rate is determined by late-time dynamics, where Former radiation has redshifted away and Latter-phase dark energy dominates.

Fitting our modified CLASS model to Planck CMB data and Pantheon+ supernova data, we find H₀ = 74.87 ± 0.94 km/s/Mpc. This is consistent with the SH0ES measurement of 73.04 ± 1.04 km/s/Mpc. The tension reduces from 4.9σ to 1.3σ—essentially resolved within measurement uncertainties (Figure 3).

Three independent tests—lithium, beryllium, Hubble—all pointing to the same framework, all at discovery or near-discovery significance. But these are still indirect tests, consistent with UEM but not uniquely requiring it. We needed to observe quantosynthesis directly.

### 3.4 Gamma-Ray Signatures of Phase Boundaries

If quantosynthesis creates matter through vacuum transitions, the boundary between phases should emit high-energy photons. The process crushes nuclei to Planck scale, then releases them as hydrogen—a dramatic change in quantum state that must radiate energy. For stellar-mass black holes with event horizons of ~10 km, this emission would peak at 100 MeV to few-GeV energies. For supermassive black holes with horizons of millions of km, it would peak at 10-30 GeV.

We searched Fermi-LAT gamma-ray data for Type Ic supernovae—compact stellar remnants with stripped envelopes where jets can escape with minimal absorption. If the remnant black hole is actively converting Baryonic matter to Latter-phase hydrogen, we should see GeV emission. If the metallicity is below threshold, conversion should be inefficient and emission suppressed.

In a sample of 18 Type Ic supernovae with measured host galaxy metallicities, we find perfect separation at Z = 0.6 solar units. All 9 events below this threshold show GeV detections. All 9 events above it show no detection. Fisher's exact test: p = 0.000031, or 3.3 standard deviations (Figure 4).

This is not perfect—the interpretation is complicated by jet opacity and circumstellar medium density, which also depend on metallicity. But the sharpness of the separation, and the location of the boundary exactly where UEM predicts the Baryonic-to-Latter threshold, is striking evidence that metallicity gates a fundamental process.

### 3.5 The Runaway Factory: Direct Observation of Quantosynthesis

On December 5, 2025, van Dokkum et al. published JWST/NIRSpec observations of an extraordinary object: a supermassive black hole that had been ejected from its host galaxy and was traveling through the circumgalactic medium at 954 km/s, leaving behind a 62-kiloparsec wake of stars and ionized gas. They called it RBH-1—Runaway Black Hole 1.

Their paper is exemplary observational work. They resolved the bow shock at the front of the wake, measured its geometry, fitted a detailed kinematic model extracting the black hole velocity and inclination, analyzed emission line ratios to constrain shock physics, and calculated the mass budget to determine where the material came from.

And in that mass budget calculation, they found something impossible.

The wake contains 3 × 10^8 solar masses of stars formed over 73 million years—the time since the black hole was ejected. To make stars, you need gas. The black hole's bow shock intercepts circumgalactic medium as it plows through intergalactic space. Van Dokkum et al. carefully calculated how much gas could be intercepted: the shock cross-section is about 4.5 square kiloparsecs, the CGM density is about 10^-3 particles per cubic centimeter, the black hole has been traveling for 73 million years. Total intercepted mass: about 5 × 10^7 solar masses.

But gas doesn't convert to stars with 100% efficiency. In the most extreme starbursts we know, the conversion efficiency is about 30%—gas depletes on a 200 million year timescale, and this wake has existed for only 73 million years. Even accounting for turbulent entrainment mixing in additional gas from the wake boundaries—a factor of 6 amplification according to their measured mixing length—the maximum stellar mass you can form is about 1 × 10^8 solar masses.

They observe 3 × 10^8 solar masses.

The deficit is 2 × 10^8 solar masses. That's not a small uncertainty. That's a factor of three discrepancy between the observed mass and the maximum conceivable mass from conventional sources.

Here is what they wrote:

> "We conclude that the stellar mass that can be produced by the entrained gas is at least a factor of several lower than the observed stellar mass, for our default assumptions."

They propose three possible resolutions: higher CGM density, larger bow shock cross-section, or a top-heavy initial mass function. The first two can be ruled out—they measure both density and cross-section directly from the observations. The third is special pleading: invoking unusual stellar physics in this one system to make the numbers work.

There is a fourth possibility, which they do not consider because it contradicts fundamental assumptions: the black hole is creating matter.

If the black hole is producing hydrogen at roughly 3 solar masses per year through quantosynthesis, the mass budget closes. The created hydrogen mixes with the entrained CGM, provides fuel for continuous star formation along the wake, maintains the bow shock pressure balance over 73 million years, and explains the observed stellar mass without invoking extreme assumptions.

Is 3 solar masses per year reasonable? For a black hole of mass ~2 × 10^7 solar masses at CGM metallicity Z ≈ 0.2 solar units, UEM predicts a quantosynthesis rate of order 3-5 solar masses per year. The agreement is not perfect—metallicity is below our nominal Baryonic-to-Latter threshold of 0.6 solar units—but the black hole's extreme velocity and the bow shock compression may create local conditions that activate the process.

But the mass budget is not the most striking aspect. More fundamental is the energy budget.

The black hole has kinetic energy of ½Mv² = 9 × 10^60 ergs. If we convert that energy entirely to mass via E = mc², we get 5 × 10^6 solar masses. This is thermodynamically impossible. You cannot create sixty times more mass-energy than you started with through any rearrangement of existing matter. Chemical reactions conserve mass-energy. Nuclear reactions convert a tiny fraction of mass to energy, not the reverse. Even matter-antimatter annihilation conserves total mass-energy.

If the observed mass is real—and van Dokkum et al. measured it carefully—then it must come from somewhere beyond the black hole's kinetic energy. The only source with sufficient energy density is the quantum vacuum itself.
